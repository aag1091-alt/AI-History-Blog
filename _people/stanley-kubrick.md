Born in New York City on July 26, 1928, Stanley Kubrick was a self-taught filmmaker who rose from photographing stories for Look magazine to commanding some of the most ambitious productions in cinema history. His films—nearly all adaptations of novels or short stories—were celebrated for their groundbreaking cinematography, obsessive attention to detail, and dark humor, cementing his legacy as one of the most influential directors of the post-war era. He died on March 7, 1999, leaving behind a body of work that continues to shape filmmaking to this day.
