# The Dartmouth Conference: AI Gets Its Name

The Dartmouth Conference in 1956 marked the birth of artificial intelligence as a scientific discipline. John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, and Nathaniel Rochester organized the event, which brought together a group of mathematicians and scientists to explore the possibility of creating machines that could simulate human intelligence.

**What happened:** In the summer of 1956, a group of pioneering scientists gathered at Dartmouth College for a six to eight-week workshop. The organizers, including John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, and Nathaniel Rochester, aimed to explore the question of whether machines could be made to simulate aspects of human intelligence. This event is widely recognized as the founding moment of artificial intelligence as a field of study. McCarthy coined the term 'Artificial Intelligence' during this conference, which would go on to define the research agenda for the next three decades.

**Why it matters:** The Dartmouth Conference set the stage for the development of AI as a distinct academic discipline. The attendees, who would become some of the most influential figures in AI history, laid the groundwork for the field's foundational theories and methodologies. Their discussions and subsequent research have had a lasting impact on the development of AI technologies and continue to influence contemporary AI research.
