# DARPA Grand Challenge: The Race That Launched Self-Driving Cars
On October 8, 2005, the DARPA Grand Challenge saw the first successful completion of an autonomous vehicle race, marking a pivotal moment in the history of AI and robotics.

**What happened:** The second DARPA Grand Challenge took place on October 8, 2005, with a 212 km (132 mi) off-road course near the California/Nevada border. Unlike the previous year's race, which saw no vehicles complete the course, five vehicles successfully navigated the challenging terrain, including Stanford's Stanley, led by Sebastian Thrun. The race required vehicles to pass through three narrow tunnels and navigate over 100 sharp turns, culminating in Beer Bottle Pass, a winding mountain pass with steep drop-offs. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DARPA_Grand_Challenge_%282005%29)

**Why it matters:** This event was a significant milestone in the development of autonomous vehicles. Stanford's Stanley, which won the competition, used a combination of LIDAR, GPS, and machine learning techniques to navigate the course. The success of the 2005 race directly contributed to the founding of Waymo and Uber ATG, and it marked the beginning of the modern autonomous vehicle industry. Sebastian Thrun, a key figure in the race, went on to lead Google's self-driving car project, further cementing the importance of this event in the history of AI. As Thrun himself noted, "No one had ever built a robot that could drive itself through unrehearsed, unknown terrain. We didn't know if it was possible."
