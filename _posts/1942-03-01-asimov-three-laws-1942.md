# Asimov's Three Laws of Robotics
In 1942, Isaac Asimov introduced the Three Laws of Robotics, a foundational ethical framework for artificial intelligence that continues to influence discussions on AI safety and ethics today.

**What happened:** In his 1942 short story "Runaround," science fiction author Isaac Asimov introduced the Three Laws of Robotics, which were later compiled and expanded upon in his 1950 collection "I, Robot." These laws stipulate that robots must not harm humans, must obey human orders, and must protect themselves, provided these actions do not conflict with the first two laws. [Wikipedia — Three Laws of Robotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_Laws_of_Robotics)

**Why it matters:** Asimov's Three Laws are significant because they represent one of the earliest attempts to address the ethical implications of artificial intelligence. They have been cited by AI researchers, ethicists, and engineers for over eight decades, highlighting the enduring relevance of Asimov's foresight. However, the inherent contradictions within the laws also underscore the complexity of ensuring that machines serve and protect humans without causing unintended harm.
