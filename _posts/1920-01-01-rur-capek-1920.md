# R.U.R. — The Word 'Robot' is Born
In 1920, the word 'robot' was introduced to the world through Karel Čapek's play 'R.U.R.,' marking the beginning of robots in literature and the public imagination.

**What happened:** In 1920, Czech writer Karel Čapek published the play 'R.U.R.,' which stands for 'Rossum's Universal Robots.' The play premiered on January 2, 1921, and quickly gained international acclaim, being translated into thirty languages by 1923. The term 'robot' in the play's title comes from the Czech word 'robota,' meaning forced labor. [Wikipedia — R.U.R.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R.U.R.)

**Why it matters:** 'R.U.R.' not only introduced the word 'robot' to the global vocabulary but also set the stage for future narratives about artificial beings and their relationship with humanity. The play's exploration of robots as artificial workers who eventually revolt against their human creators has become a recurring theme in science fiction, influencing countless stories about AI uprisings and the ethics of creating sentient machines.
