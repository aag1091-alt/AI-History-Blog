In 1770, Wolfgang von Kempelen unveiled the Mechanical Turk, a chess-playing automaton that captivated audiences across Europe and America for over eight decades, only to be revealed as a clever hoax.

**What happened:** In 1770, Wolfgang von Kempelen, an inventor and polymath from Bratislava, introduced the Mechanical Turk, a chess-playing machine that appeared to be an autonomous automaton. The Turk toured Europe and America, playing against notable figures such as Napoleon Bonaparte and Benjamin Franklin, and defeating them with ease. Despite its impressive performance, the Turk was actually a sophisticated hoax, concealing a human chess master within its elaborate cabinet. [Wikipedia — Turk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turk)

**Why it matters:** The Mechanical Turk's longevity and success in fooling audiences planted the seed of the idea that machines could think and make decisions, influencing the development of artificial intelligence. Today, the legacy of the Turk lives on in modern technology, with Amazon's Mechanical Turk platform named after it, acknowledging that even in the age of AI, human labor often remains hidden behind the scenes.
