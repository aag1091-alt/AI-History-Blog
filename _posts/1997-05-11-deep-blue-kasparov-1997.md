# The Day a Machine Dethroned the King of Chess (1997)

On May 11, 1997, Garry Kasparov, the reigning world chess champion, was defeated by IBM's Deep Blue computer in a six-game match, marking a pivotal moment in the history of artificial intelligence.

**What happened:** In 1997, Deep Blue, a chess-playing computer developed by Feng-hsiung Hsu and his team at IBM, faced off against Garry Kasparov in a rematch of their 1996 encounter. This time, Deep Blue emerged victorious, winning two games, drawing three, and losing one. The match was a significant milestone in the field of AI, demonstrating the capabilities of machines to outperform human experts in a complex intellectual domain. [Deep Blue (chess computer)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_Blue_(chess_computer)) [Deep Blue versus Garry Kasparov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_Blue_versus_Garry_Kasparov)

**Why it matters:** Deep Blue's victory over Kasparov was not just a triumph of technology but also a cultural landmark that reshaped public perception of artificial intelligence. It sparked debates about the nature of intelligence, creativity, and the potential of machines to 'think.' The match highlighted the rapid advancement of AI and its ability to solve complex problems, setting the stage for future developments in the field.
