# Deep Blue Defeats Garry Kasparov
On May 11, 1997, Deep Blue, a supercomputer developed by Feng-hsiung Hsu and his team at IBM, defeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov in a six-game match, marking a pivotal moment in the history of artificial intelligence.

**What happened:** In 1997, Deep Blue, a customized IBM RS/6000 SP supercomputer designed specifically for playing chess, faced off against Garry Kasparov, the reigning world champion. After an initial match in 1996 where Kasparov emerged victorious, Deep Blue underwent significant upgrades and returned to challenge Kasparov again. This time, Deep Blue won two games, drew three, and secured a historic victory. The project was initially conceived at Carnegie Mellon University under the name ChipTest, later renamed Deep Thought, and finally Deep Blue in 1989. Murray Campbell, along with Feng-hsiung Hsu and other IBM researchers, played crucial roles in the development and refinement of Deep Blue. [Wikipedia — Deep Blue (chess computer)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_Blue_(chess_computer))

**Why it matters:** The defeat of Kasparov by Deep Blue was a landmark event that demonstrated the power of brute-force computational methods in solving complex problems once thought to require human-like intelligence. Chess had long been a benchmark for AI research, symbolizing the pinnacle of strategic thinking and decision-making. Deep Blue's victory not only challenged the notion that chess mastery required human-like intuition but also sparked debates about the nature of intelligence and the limits of computational power. This event underscored the rapid advancements in AI during the Neural Network Renaissance era (1989–2011).

Kasparov's reaction to the defeat encapsulated the profound impact of this moment: "It was like playing against an alien." This statement highlighted the uncanny and almost incomprehensible nature of Deep Blue's performance, marking a new era in the relationship between humans and machines in the realm of intelligence and problem-solving.
