# Frankenstein: The First AI Nightmare

Frankenstein, published in 1818, is a seminal work that introduced the world to the dangers of unchecked scientific ambition, a theme that resonates deeply with contemporary discussions about artificial intelligence.

**What happened:** In 1818, Mary Shelley, at the tender age of 20, anonymously published *Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus*. The novel tells the story of Victor Frankenstein, a young scientist who creates a sapient creature from disparate body parts, only to see his creation spiral out of control. Shelley's inspiration for the novel came from her travels through Europe, particularly along the Rhine and in Geneva, where she pondered the implications of scientific and philosophical inquiries of her time. [Wikipedia — Frankenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankenstein)

**Why it matters:** *Frankenstein* is a foundational text in the discourse on artificial life and the ethical implications of playing God. Shelley's narrative explores the consequences of a creator's failure to foresee the repercussions of their creation, a theme that continues to be relevant in discussions about the development and regulation of AI. The novel's enduring influence lies in its cautionary tale about the perils of unchecked technological advancement and the moral responsibility of those who wield such power.
