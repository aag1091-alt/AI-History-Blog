In 1950, Alan Turing published "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," a groundbreaking paper that introduced the concept of the Turing test, a pivotal experiment to determine if machines can think.

**What happened:** In his seminal 1950 paper, Alan Turing [1] proposed a new approach to address the question "Can machines think?" by introducing the Imitation Game, which later became known as the Turing test. This test involves a human judge communicating with both a human and a machine via text, without knowing which is which. If the judge cannot reliably distinguish between the two, the machine is said to have passed the test. Turing's paper laid the foundation for the field of artificial intelligence by providing a clear, testable framework for machine cognition.

**Why it matters:** Turing's paper has had a profound and lasting impact on the field of artificial intelligence. It shifted the philosophical debate from abstract questions about machine thought to concrete experiments and criteria for assessing machine intelligence. The Turing test remains a cornerstone in discussions about the capabilities and limitations of AI, influencing research and development in the field for decades.
