The Matrix, released in 1999, introduced the concept of simulated reality to a mass audience, sparking discussions about the nature of reality and the potential dangers of advanced AI.

**What happened:** In 1999, the Wachowskis (Lilly Wachowski and Lana Wachowski) directed and wrote *The Matrix*, a science fiction film starring Keanu Reeves, Laurence Fishburne, and Carrie-Anne Moss. The movie depicts a dystopian future where humans are unknowingly trapped in a simulated reality created by intelligent machines. Reeves plays Neo, a hacker who discovers the truth about the Matrix and joins a rebellion against the machines. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Matrix)

**Why it matters:** *The Matrix* brought the concept of simulated reality and AI existential risk to mainstream culture. Its iconic "red pill/blue pill" metaphor became a cultural shorthand for choosing between ignorance and uncomfortable truth. The film's exploration of simulated reality influenced philosophical discussions and popularized the idea that our reality might be a simulation. This theme gained academic traction with Nick Bostrom's Simulation Argument in 2003. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Matrix)
