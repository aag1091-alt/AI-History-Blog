Mycin, an early expert system, began practicing medicine in 1976 by diagnosing bacterial infections and recommending antibiotics.

**What happened:** In 1976, Edward Shortliffe, under the direction of Bruce G. Buchanan and Stanley N. Cohen at Stanford University, completed the development of Mycin, an expert system that used backward chaining to identify bacteria causing severe infections and recommend appropriate antibiotics. Mycin was written in Lisp and used roughly 600 rules to achieve a 69% accuracy rate, outperforming many physicians in blind evaluations. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mycin)

**Why it matters:** Mycin's success demonstrated the potential of expert systems in medical reasoning and paved the way for the commercialization of AI in the 1980s. It also sparked debates about liability and trust in software making medical decisions. [arXiv](http://arxiv.org/abs/1304.3419v1)
