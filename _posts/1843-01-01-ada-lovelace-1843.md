**The Birth of AI Philosophy**

Ada Lovelace's notes on Charles Babbage's Analytical Engine in 1843 contain the first algorithm and laid the foundation for modern computer programming.

**What happened:** Ada Lovelace recognized the machine's potential beyond pure calculation, writing "The Analytical Engine has no power of originating anything. It can only do what we know how to order it to perform." This concept, known as the Lovelace objection, still influences AI debates today. [Ada Lovelace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ada_Lovelace) was a visionary mathematician who envisioned machines doing more than just calculations. Her ideas also sparked debate on whether machines can originate new ideas. A recent arXiv paper [Comment on: Was Ada Lovelace Actually the First Programmer?](http://arxiv.org/abs/2402.00749v1) revisits this question and its relevance to AI.

**Why it matters:** Lovelace's work enabled future generations of programmers, including pioneers like Alan Turing, who built upon her ideas in his work on machine learning and artificial intelligence. Her vision also highlights the ongoing discussion between humans and machines about creativity and innovation.
