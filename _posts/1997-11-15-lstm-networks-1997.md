# LSTM: Teaching Machines to Remember

On November 15, 1997, Sepp Hochreiter and Jürgen Schmidhuber introduced the Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) model, a breakthrough in neural network architecture that addressed a critical challenge in machine learning.

**What happened:** In 1997, Sepp Hochreiter and Jürgen Schmidhuber published a paper introducing LSTM, a type of recurrent neural network (RNN) designed to solve the vanishing gradient problem that plagued traditional RNNs. LSTM units include a cell and three gates (input, output, and forget) that regulate the flow of information, allowing the network to remember information over long periods. [Wikipedia — Long short-term memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long_short-term_memory)

**Why it matters:** Before LSTM, RNNs struggled to learn patterns that spanned more than a few time steps, severely limiting their practical applications. LSTM's gated memory architecture enabled neural networks to handle long-term dependencies, making it a cornerstone for advancements in speech recognition, machine translation, and text generation. For nearly two decades, LSTM was the go-to solution until transformers emerged as a superior alternative. [arXiv — 2021](http://arxiv.org/abs/2104.05712v2)

**Further reading:**

Sepp Hochreiter once noted, "The problem of vanishing gradients had been a roadblock. LSTM was designed to overcome it by design, not by tricks." This insight paved the way for a new era in neural network research and applications.
